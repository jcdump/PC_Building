Starting a PC Build

Before starting to build a desktop pc, it is good to have a case in mind, if not a case, a case size.Your case size will determine what components can fit in your build and what cannot. The best PC size for you will depend on your needs, budget, and preferences. Take time to research and compare different options before making a decision.

Motherboards

Motherboards serve as the backbone of a computer system, providing connectivity and compatibility for various components such as the CPU, memory, storage devices, graphics cards, and peripherals. Choosing the right motherboard is crucial for building a stable and high-performance desktop PC. Motherboards come in different form factors, including ATX, Micro-ATX (mATX), and Mini-ITX. The form factor determines the physical size of the motherboard and its compatibility with different PC cases. When choosing a desktop motherboard, consider factors such as form factor, CPU socket compatibility, chipset features, memory support, expansion slots, storage connectivity, I/O ports, networking and audio capabilities, build quality, price, and budget. Researching reviews, comparisons, and user feedback can help you find the best motherboard for your specific PC build and requirements.

Socket

The CPU socket determines the type of processor that can be installed on the motherboard. Common CPU sockets for desktop motherboards include Intel's LGA (Land Grid Array) sockets, such as LGA 1200 for 10th and 11th generation Intel Core processors, LGA 1700 for 12th to 14th generation Intel Core processors, AMD's AM4 socket for Ryzen Zen+, Zen 2 and Zen 3 processors, and AMDâ€™s AM5 socket for Ryzen Zen 4(+) processors. Ensure compatibility between the motherboard's CPU socket and your CPU.

Chipset

The chipset is a crucial component on the motherboard that facilitates communication between the CPU, memory, storage devices, and other peripherals. Different chipsets offer varying features and connectivity options, such as support for overclocking, PCIe lanes for expansion cards, USB and SATA ports, and integrated networking capabilities. Choose a motherboard with a chipset that meets your specific requirements and budget.

Processors

Choosing the right desktop processor is crucial for achieving optimal performance in various tasks, including gaming, content creation, productivity, and everyday computing. The two main CPU manufacturers for desktop processors are Intel and AMD. Both offer a range of processors targeting different performance levels and price points. It is best to do some research to find what price:performance point suits your requirements and budget in the current market. Performance, price, core count, threads, socket compatibility, integrated graphics, use case, and future upgrade-ability are all things to consider when choosing a CPU.

Memory

Memory, also known as RAM (Random Access Memory), is a crucial component of a computer system that temporarily stores data and instructions that the CPU needs to access quickly. Choosing the right memory modules is essential for ensuring smooth multitasking, fast application loading times, and overall system performance. The most common type of desktop memory is DDR (Double Data Rate) SDRAM (Synchronous Dynamic Random Access Memory). DDR5 is the latest iteration of DDR memory, succeeding DDR4. It brings several enhancements over DDR4, including higher data transfer rates, improved power efficiency, and increased capacity potential. Most systems will use either DDR4 or DDR5, although some older systems may still use DDR3. When choosing between DDR4 and DDR5 memory, consider your performance requirements, budget, motherboard compatibility, future-proofing needs, usage scenario, CPU compatibility, and price-performance ratio. DDR4 is a solid choice for most users with standard computing needs, while DDR5 offers cutting-edge performance for enthusiasts, gamers, content creators, and professionals who demand the best performance from their system

DDR4

Speed
DDR4 memory typically ranges from 2133 MHz to 4800 MHz (with overclocking support for higher speeds).
Provides fast data transfer rates, offering responsive performance for most computing tasks.
Data Rate
DDR4 transfers data on both the rising and falling edges of the clock signal, resulting in a data rate of 4 transfers per clock cycle.
Capacity
DDR4 modules are available in capacities ranging from 4GB to 64GB per module.
Higher capacities are more common as the technology matures.
Voltage
DDR4 memory typically operates at a standard voltage of 1.2V.
Helps reduce power consumption and heat generation.
Architecture
DDR4 memory is based on a 4-bit prefetch architecture, fetching 4 pieces of data per clock cycle.
Offers good performance and efficiency for a wide range of applications.
Usage
Suitable for mainstream desktops, gaming rigs, workstations, and servers.
Provides good performance for everyday computing tasks, gaming, content creation, and multitasking.

DDR5

Speed:
DDR5 memory speeds start from around 4800 MHz and can go up to 8400 MHz or higher.
Provides significantly faster data transfer rates compared to DDR4.
Data Rate:
DDR5 transfers data on the rising and falling edges of both the command and data signals, doubling the data rate.
Achieves a data rate of 8 transfers per clock cycle, leading to increased bandwidth and performance.
Capacity:
DDR5 modules are expected to offer higher densities and capacities compared to DDR4.
Can potentially offer capacities of 32GB, 64GB, 128GB, and higher.
Voltage:
DDR5 memory is designed to operate at a lower voltage of 1.1V.
Improves energy efficiency and reduces power consumption further compared to DDR4.
Architecture:
DDR5 memory utilizes a 16-bit prefetch architecture, fetching 16 pieces of data per clock cycle.
Improves memory efficiency and bandwidth, resulting in better overall performance.
Usage:
Ideal for high-end desktops, gaming PCs, content creation workstations, and servers.
Offers superior performance for demanding applications, gaming, 3D rendering, AI, and data-intensive tasks.

CPU Coolers

CPU coolers are essential components that dissipate heat generated by the CPU, ensuring stable and optimal operating temperatures. They come in various designs and sizes, ranging from air coolers to liquid coolers. When choosing a desktop CPU cooler, consider factors such as cooling performance, compatibility with your CPU socket and motherboard, form factor, overclocking capability, noise levels, budget, and aesthetics. Researching reviews and benchmarks can help you find the best CPU cooler for your specific needs and preferences.

Air Coolers

Form:
Use metal heatsinks and fans to dissipate heat away from the CPU.
Components:
Typically consist of a heatsink with heat pipes and one or more fans.
Variety:
Come in various sizes and designs, with larger heatsinks and more fans generally providing better cooling performance.
Performance Factors:
Can depend on factors such as fan speed, airflow, and heat pipe design.
Sizes:
Available in different sizes to fit various CPU sockets and form factors.
Compatibility:
Ensure compatibility with your CPU socket and motherboard, considering factors such as clearance for RAM modules and other components.

Liquid Coolers

Form:
Uses a closed-loop system or custom loop to transfer heat away from the CPU using a liquid coolant.
Components:
Consist of a pump, radiator, tubing, and water block (or CPU block) with a copper or nickel-plated base.
Performance:
Typically offer excellent cooling performance, especially in scenarios with high CPU loads or overclocking.
Heat Dissipation:
Can effectively dissipate heat and maintain lower temperatures compared to air coolers.
Sizes:
Come in different sizes, typically with radiator sizes ranging from 120mm to 360mm or larger.
Compatibility:
Ensure compatibility with your PC case for radiator mounting and clearance, as well as compatibility with your CPU socket.

Low Profile

Form:
Designed for compact PC builds with limited space, such as small form factor (SFF) PCs or HTPCs.
Features:
Feature a compact design with a low profile to fit into tight spaces while still providing adequate cooling.
Performance:
While may not offer the same cooling performance as larger air or liquid coolers, they are sufficient for cooling most mainstream CPUs under normal operating conditions.
Compatibility:
Check compatibility with your PC case and motherboard to ensure proper fitment, especially in compact or low-profile builds.

Power Supplies

Power supplies, often referred to as PSUs (Power Supply Units), are critical components responsible for converting AC (alternating current) power from the wall outlet into DC (direct current) power required by the computer's components. Choosing the right power supply is essential for providing stable and reliable power to your system while ensuring compatibility with your components and allowing for future upgrades. When choosing a desktop power supply, consider factors such as wattage, efficiency rating, modularity, connectors and cable lengths, form factor, quality and reliability, cooling and noise levels, price, and budget. Researching reviews, comparisons, and user feedback can help you find the best power supply for your specific system configuration and requirements

Storage

Desktop storage refers to the various types of storage devices used in desktop computers to store data, applications, and operating systems. Common types of desktop storage include hard disk drives (HDDs), solid-state drives (SSDs), and optical drives (such as CD/DVD drives). When choosing desktop storage devices, consider factors such as performance, capacity, reliability, budget, usage scenario, and future upgradability. SSDs offer superior performance and faster data access speeds compared to HDDs, while HDDs provide higher capacities at lower costs. Assess your specific requirements and prioritize features based on your needs and budget constraints.

HDDs

Form:
Store data on spinning magnetic disks (platters) and use read/write heads to access and modify data.
Available in various form factors, including 3.5-inch and 2.5-inch drives, with larger capacities typically found in 3.5-inch drives.
Storage Capacities:
Offer larger storage capacities compared to SSDs, making them suitable for storing large files, multimedia content, and backups.
Capacities can range from a few hundred gigabytes to multiple terabytes.
Affordability:
Generally more affordable on a per-gigabyte basis compared to SSDs, making them an economical choice for bulk storage and secondary drives.
Performance:
While HDDs offer high capacities at relatively low prices, they are slower than SSDs in terms of data transfer rates and access times.
Suitable For:
Suitable for storing large files that don't require frequent access or high-speed performance.

SSDs

Form:
Use flash memory to store data, providing faster data access and transfer speeds compared to HDDs.
Available in various form factors, including 2.5-inch SATA, M.2, and PCIe NVMe drives.
Storage Capacities:
Capacities ranging from a few hundred gigabytes to multiple terabytes, with higher-capacity options available for users requiring ample storage space.
Affordability:
Prices have been decreasing in recent years, making SSDs more affordable for mainstream users.
While generally more expensive per gigabyte compared to HDDs, their performance benefits often justify the higher cost for many users.
Performance:
Offer significantly faster read and write speeds compared to HDDs, resulting in improved system responsiveness, faster boot times, and reduced application load times.
Suitable for:
Primary system drives (boot drives).
Storing frequently accessed files and applications
